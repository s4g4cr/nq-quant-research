Research summary

Better geometry, same outcome — a fragile training edge

Phase 17 established that the opening spike extreme is a real behavioral anchor — price reverts to it 93.3% of the time. Phase 18 tested whether that level acts as durable support/resistance on subsequent retests, with the critical fix of decoupling the stop loss from the spike extreme itself.

The ATR-based SL (1.0× ATR_1min at entry bar) immediately resolved the geometry problem identified in Phase 17. Median R/R climbed to 1.78 for TP-A and 3.09 for TP-B, versus 0.50 and 0.86 in Phase 17. Theoretical expectancy turned marginally positive (+0.022 for TP-A). All 9 experiments were able to run — the diagnostic gate was cleared for the first time in the spike series.

Training period (2021–2024) results were marginally profitable: PF 1.044, SR 0.344, WR 35.7% (Exp 1 baseline). But the OOS test period (2025–2026) showed a sharp WR collapse to 23.4%, driving all configurations to PF < 1.0. The best combined experiment (Exp 9: spike>3×ATR, min R/R=2.0) reached only PF 0.915 on 90 test trades — insufficient to trigger walk-forward validation.

What changed in 2025–2026: Broader ATRs in 2025 increased SL distances while TP-A (session_open) remained a fixed geometric target. Entries that previously had comfortable room to session_open now required a larger adverse excursion before reaching TP. Higher volatility amplified the gap between theoretical expectancy (computed on full dataset) and live hit rate. The signal correctly identifies the behavioral pattern; the cost structure under wide ATR regimes is unfavorable.

Phase 18 · Rejected

Opening Spike Extreme as Support/Resistance

Phase 18 directly addressed Phase 17's root cause. Instead of entering after a spike and placing the SL at the spike extreme, Phase 18 enters when price returns to the spike extreme and shows rejection — with an ATR-based SL that is independent of spike geometry.

Spike detection — 09:30 to 09:34

Same spike detection as Phase 17 with one change: ties (up_move == down_move) go to "up" direction instead of being skipped. All sessions now produce a SpikeInfo — direction filter is applied to trading logic, not detection.

Spike classification
1session_open = open price of 09:30 bar
2spike_high / spike_low = max high / min low across 09:30–09:34
3up_move = spike_high − session_open · down_move = session_open − spike_low
4direction = "up" if up_move ≥ down_move, "down" otherwise (ties → "up")
5Size filter: spike_magnitude > 1.5× ATR(20) [1-min Wilder ATR] — applied in run()

Entry rules — 09:35 to 11:30

For a down spike (spike extreme = spike_low), we look for LONG entries when price returns to within 0.5×ATR of the spike low and shows a rejection bar. The critical difference from Phase 17: SL is 1.0×ATR at the entry bar, not spike_low. This decouples SL distance from spike geometry.

Long entry conditions (down spike · 7 conditions)
01Bar low ≤ spike_low + 0.5× ATR_1min — price entered the retest zone
02Bar close > spike_low — closes above the extreme (rejection)
03Bar close > bar open — bullish bar
04Bar close > bar low + 0.5×(bar high − bar low) — upper 50% of range
05Bar volume > 1.2× avg_volume(20) — elevated participation
06Bar close < session_open — still below open (TP-A room intact)
SLentry − 1.0× ATR_1min at entry bar (fixed, ATR-based, not spike-based)
RRHard gate: skip if (TP − entry) / sl_pts < 1.0
Max90 bars (1.5 hrs) · EOD 15:45 · up to 2 entries per session (sequential)

Three TP variants tested

Target options (LONG example — down spike)
Asession_open — conservative; median R/R 1.78 in diagnostic
Bsession_open + 0.5×(session_open − spike_low) — extended; median R/R 3.09
Cspike_high (opposite extreme) — full reversion; median R/R 2.56
TTrailing stop (Exp 4) — activates at 1.0×ATR profit, trails 0.75×ATR from peak

Why the geometry fix wasn't enough

R/R geometry was genuinely fixed. The diagnostic showed median R/R of 1.78–3.09 depending on TP variant, and theoretical expectancy was marginally positive. The training period (2021–2024) confirmed this with PF 1.044.

The win rate problem: In the OOS test period (2025–2026), win rate dropped from 35.7% to 23.4% (Exp 1). With ATR-sized SL, the break-even WR for R/R=2.76 (Exp 1 average) is only 27%. The 23.4% OOS WR still falls below that threshold. Higher 2025 volatility increased ATR, widening SL distances while TP-A (session_open) remained the same distance away — shrinking effective R/R per trade and pushing real break-even WR upward. The signal fires on the right bars; the market no longer follows through at the required rate.

What we learned

The spike series: two falsifications, one convergence

Phases 17 and 18 together mapped the boundary of what can and cannot be extracted from NQ opening spike behavior using systematic confirmation-based entry. Both phases tested real and documented behavioral patterns. Both were ultimately insufficient.

What Phase 17 and 18 confirm

The opening 5-minute spike in NQ is a consistent, measurable phenomenon. 85–86% of sessions produce a spike larger than 1.5× the 1-minute ATR. Price returns to the spike extreme in 93% of qualifying sessions. The level acts as a genuine magnetic anchor for the first two hours of trading.

This is consistent with widely observed market behavior: the opening spike sweeps one side of liquidity, trapping participants, and the subsequent reversal is fueled by those exits. The pattern is real. The question was whether a systematic entry can capture it profitably.

Phase 17 → Phase 18: what the fix accomplished

Phase 17 — Geometry failure

SL at spike extreme. Entry between SL and TP. Median R/R 0.50 (TP-A), 0.86 (TP-B). Structural negative expectancy — no parameter can fix it. Diagnostic failed.

Phase 18 — WR failure

ATR-based SL. Median R/R 1.78 (TP-A), 3.09 (TP-B). Diagnostic passed. But OOS WR of 23.4% vs 35.7% in training — costs still win.

Phase 18 made the correct change and it worked geometrically. The constraint shifted from "geometry is broken" to "WR is insufficient." This is progress — the failure mode changed from structural to empirical.

The OOS WR collapse — what likely explains it

2025 volatility regime shift: The test period (2025–2026) shows broader ATRs than the training period. A wider ATR means: (1) larger SL distance → more adverse excursion before stop is hit; (2) TP-A (session_open) is not proportionally farther → effective R/R shrinks; (3) the break-even WR rises. The rejection bar signal may fire at the right level but the market no longer follows through at the rate required for profitability under wider intraday ranges.

A regime-conditional version — only trading when ATR is below a threshold, or when daily range is in a favorable regime — was not tested in Phase 18. This is a viable next hypothesis if the spike series were to continue.

What to try if the series continues

Potential extensions (not tested)
1Regime filter on ATR: only trade when daily ATR is below rolling 20-day median. Avoids high-vol regimes that shrink effective R/R.
2Pre-spike entry: enter a limit at the spike extreme BEFORE price returns. Requires a prediction model (direction of next spike) — a different hypothesis.
3Scalable SL: SL = 0.5×ATR instead of 1.0×ATR. Tighter stop with same TP increases R/R — but likely lowers WR further. Needs testing.
4Session type filter: condition on overnight range, news day, or implied volatility. Spike behavior may differ across session types.

Broader lessons for the research series

The spike series produced the most informative pair of falsifications in the research. Each phase identified a specific, fixable flaw. Phase 17's flaw (geometry) was fixed. Phase 18's flaw (OOS WR) points at regime sensitivity rather than a logical error. This is the normal progression of systematic research: hypothesis → explicit failure → refined hypothesis.

The POC Reversion strategy (Phase 15 B1) remains the only confirmed edge in the series: p=0.066, bootstrap p5=0.999, 4/5 WFO windows PF >1.0. The spike series confirms that not all real behavioral patterns are extractable with systematic confirmation-based entries — some patterns require prediction rather than confirmation, or are only profitable in specific volatility regimes.

Key insight from the spike series

The opening spike level is a real structural anchor (93% retest rate, 82% rejection rate). The edge, if it exists, requires either: (a) prediction of spike direction before it forms, (b) regime conditioning to avoid high-ATR periods, or (c) a fundamentally different entry mechanism than the rejection bar. Confirmation-based entry after the fact is one step too late in the current volatility environment.
