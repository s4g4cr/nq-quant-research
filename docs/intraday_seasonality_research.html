Academic replication · Phase 21

The Heston et al. (2010) intraday seasonality hypothesis

Heston, Korajczyk & Sadka (2010) — published in the Journal of Finance — documented that the return of a stock in a specific 30-minute interval predicts its return in the same interval on the following 40 trading days. The effect is driven by predictable institutional order flows that repeat at fixed intraday times.

The mechanism: large institutions that execute recurring programs (pension fund rebalancing, index replication, algorithmic strategies with fixed execution windows) create predictable price pressure at the same times each day. If the 14:00–14:30 window is dominated by pension fund selling on Tuesdays, that selling pattern repeats — and the sign of the 14:00–14:30 return over the last N days carries information about what the next N days will look like.

Phase 21 tests whether the same seasonality exists in NQ E-mini futures. NQ is a very different instrument from individual equities: it is a macro aggregate with diverse participant types (HFT, systematic CTAs, options hedgers, retail). The hypothesis is that if any large participant segment exhibits timing-predictable behavior, it should appear in the autocorrelation structure of the 30-minute returns.

Methodology

Experimental design
113 intervals tested: I1 (09:30–10:00) through I13 (15:30–16:00). Each interval = exactly one 30-minute RTH period.
2Signal: sign(mean return of interval over last N days). Positive mean → predict positive return; negative mean → predict negative return.
3Entry: at the open of the interval in the predicted direction. One contract. No SL/TP — hard exit at interval close.
4N lookback values: [1, 3, 5, 10, 20] days — 5 configurations per interval = 65 combinations total.
5Best interval selection: per walk-forward window, select the interval+N combo with highest SR on the training data. Apply that selection to the OOS window.
6No slippage/commission in Part A — theoretical test first. Costs applied in WFO validation.

Phase 21 · Diagnostic

The effect exists in NQ — but it shifts across time

Autocorrelation analysis of the 13 RTH intervals shows that 8 of 13 have statistically significant autocorrelation at lags consistent with the Heston et al. hypothesis. The effect is real in NQ — but the intervals that are significant change across sub-periods, and no single interval is consistently the strongest.

Selection bias is the core problem: With 65 combinations and an 18-month test period, finding SR=+1.788 for one combination is expected by chance. Walk-forward validation — where interval selection is done on training data and tested on unseen data — is the only valid test of whether the effect is real.
Walk-forward · Phase 21

Interval selection is pure in-sample overfitting

Five anchored walk-forward windows, each selecting the best interval+lookback combination on training data and applying it to an unseen 6-month OOS window. Result: 0/5 windows positive. Each window picks a different best interval. The I10+N10 in-sample result is selection bias.

Interval instability — the core finding

The five selected intervals across WFO windows are: I1, I8, I10, I10, I5. Three different intervals selected across five windows. I10 was selected twice but performed worst in its second OOS window (V4: SR=−2.859). The interval that is "best" in any training window has no predictive power for the subsequent OOS window.

Walk-forward verdict · Phase 21

Hypothesis falsified. 0/5 OOS windows positive. Pooled PF=0.863, p=0.9199. The in-sample SR=+1.788 (I10+N10) is entirely attributable to selection bias across 65 combinations. No interval is persistently best — each training window selects a different interval, and OOS performance is uniformly negative. The Heston seasonality effect, documented in individual US equities, does not generalize to NQ futures.

Phase 21 · Conclusions

A real effect that cannot be exploited

Phase 21 answers a clean question: can the Heston et al. (2010) intraday seasonality be systematically exploited in NQ futures? The answer is no. The effect is present in the autocorrelation structure but too unstable across time periods to produce consistent OOS edge.

Confirmed by the data

Findings that stand
✓8/13 intervals show statistically significant autocorrelation at the 10-day lag in NQ. The half-hour seasonality effect exists in the data — not absent like in some instruments.
✓I10 (14:00–14:30) shows the strongest autocorrelation in the full dataset (r=+0.142, p=0.0008). The 14:00–14:30 window is mechanically plausible as a macro-driven recurring order flow window.
✓SR=+1.788 in-sample for I10+N10 on the test period. This is a large in-sample number that correctly triggered WFO.

Not confirmed — the hypothesis is falsified

Results that failed OOS validation
✗0/5 WFO windows positive (p=0.9199). The strongest possible rejection of the trading hypothesis.
✗Interval selection is completely unstable — three different intervals selected as "best" across five windows. No interval is persistently best.
✗The Heston effect documented in equities does not generalize to NQ futures. SR=+1.788 is selection bias from 65 combinations, not structural signal.

Why equities → NQ does not generalize

Likely explanation: The Heston et al. effect in individual equities is driven by specific large participants (pension funds, index funds, sector ETFs) with fixed execution windows for specific stocks. NQ as an index future aggregates hundreds of stocks and attracts a far more diverse participant mix — HFT market makers, macro CTAs, options dealers hedging gamma, retail day traders, and institutional index managers all participate with different time horizons and schedules. The averaging across participant types dilutes any single predictable order flow pattern. An individual stock might have one dominant pension fund that rebalances at 14:00 every Tuesday; NQ has no such concentration.

Final verdict · Phase 21

Hypothesis falsified. The intraday 30-minute seasonality effect documented by Heston et al. (2010) in individual US equities does not generalize to NQ E-mini futures. The autocorrelation effect is present but shifts across time — no interval is persistently predictive. Walk-forward: 0/5 windows positive, p=0.9199. The in-sample SR=+1.788 is selection bias from 65 combinations, not structural edge. This is a clean falsification of a well-specified academic hypothesis.
