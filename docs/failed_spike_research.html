One phase of systematic testing on the hypothesis that the opening 5-minute spike in NQ frequently fails and reverts. The reversion behavior is real and documented. The entry geometry is not: the position of the entry price between the SL and TP creates structural negative R/R that no parameter can overcome.

Research summary

A real phenomenon that cannot be traded profitably

The first 5 minutes of NQ trading frequently produce a sharp spike that sweeps liquidity before the real session direction is established. This is widely known and observed by practitioners. Phase 17 tested whether the failure of that spike — price reversing back toward the open — could be captured as a systematic trade.

The answer is: the reversion is real and statistically robust. 85.6% of sessions have a spike larger than 1.5× the 1-minute ATR. Of those, 71.3% return to session open within 60 bars. The median time to reversion is 4 bars (4 minutes). These numbers suggest a genuine behavioral pattern.

The strategy cannot extract value from it because of how the trade must be structured. The SL is placed beyond the spike extreme (the "failure" point). The entry occurs during the reversion — somewhere between the spike extreme and session open. This means session_open (TP-A) is always closer to entry than the spike extreme (SL). The geometry is inverted: the stop is always wider than the target.

What this research confirms about NQ: The opening spike pattern is real and observable. The behavioral insight — that trapped participants fuel a fast reversal — holds empirically. The problem is purely mechanical: the entry confirmation required to avoid false signals places the trader too close to the target and too far from the stop. A prediction-based approach (entering before the spike forms) would face a different, untested hypothesis.

Phase 17 · Rejected

Failed Opening Spike Reversion

The first 5 minutes of NQ trading (09:30–09:34) frequently produce a sharp directional move that sweeps one side of liquidity before price reverses. The hypothesis: these spikes fail at a predictable rate and can be faded with confirmation — entering on the first reversal signal after the spike extreme is set.

Spike detection — 09:30 to 09:34

A spike is defined entirely from the first 5 bars. The session open is the 09:30 open price. The spike high and spike low are the max high and min low across all 5 bars. Direction is determined by which move is larger:

Spike classification
1up_move = spike_high − session_open
2down_move = session_open − spike_low
3spike_magnitude = max(up_move, down_move)
4direction = "up" if up_move > down_move, "down" otherwise. Equal → ambiguous, skip.
5Size filter: spike_magnitude > 1.5 × ATR(20) [1-min Wilder ATR]

Entry rules — 09:35 to 09:54

For a down spike (bearish spike that should reverse upward), we look for LONG entry signals. The bar must show evidence of the reversal beginning — price recovering, staying off the spike low, with elevated volume.

Long entry (down spike reversion)
01Bar close > prior close — net buying on the bar
02Close in upper 40% of bar range — bullish body position
03Volume > 1.3 × avg_volume(20) — elevated participation
04Bar low > spike_low — not retesting the extreme
05Close < session_open — still below the origin, TP intact
SLspike_low − 0.5 pts (fixed, does not move)
TP-Asession_open (conservative)
TP-Bspike_high (full reversion to opposite extreme)
Max60 bars · EOD exit 15:45 NY · 1 trade per session

Position sizing

Percentage-of-capital sizing normalised to SL distance: n_contracts = max(1, floor((capital × 1%) / (sl_pts × $20))). Slippage: 0.5 pts per side (2 ticks — wider than usual for the open). Commission: $4.00 round-trip per contract.

Why it failed

The entry condition requires close < session_open for a LONG trade. This means the entry price is always between the spike low and session open. Session open is TP-A. The spike low minus 0.5 pts is the SL. By construction:

The geometry trap: Entry sits between SL (spike extreme) and TP-A (session_open). The closer to session_open the entry is, the smaller TP-A and the larger the SL. Median TP-A distance: 15.5 pts. Median SL distance: 30.2 pts. The R/R is approximately 0.50 at the median — you need a 67% win rate to break even. Actual win rate: 63.5%. Even TP-B (spike_high, the far opposite extreme at median 26.2 pts) only moves the median R/R to 0.86, still below 1.0, with 52.2% WR.
Verdict · Phase 17

Failed spike reversion with confirmation entry has no positive theoretical expectancy across any TP configuration tested. The entry confirmation logic (bar must be below session_open for LONG) creates an irreducible geometry problem: SL is always structurally wider than TP. Experiments were gated by the mandatory pre-P&L diagnostic and never ran — negative expectancy was confirmed at the signal level before any simulation.

Phase 17 · Pre-P&L Diagnostic

Five mandatory checks before any backtest

The research spec requires a mandatory pre-P&L diagnostic to run on the full dataset before any experiments. If theoretical expectancy is negative, the script stops. This is the diagnostic that stopped Phase 17.

Per spec: experiments were never run. The diagnostic confirmed negative theoretical expectancy for both TP variants. Running the 7 planned experiments would have added costs and slippage on top of an already structurally negative signal. The diagnostic worked exactly as intended.

Research conclusions

What Phase 17 established

Phase 17 is the clearest example in this research series of a behaviorally real pattern that cannot be traded: the phenomenon exists, the methodology confirms it, and the trade structure prevents capturing it.

What is confirmed about NQ opening behavior

FindingEvidenceImplication
Opening spikes are nearly universal 85.6% of sessions produce spike >1.5× ATR in first 5 bars Not a rare or special event — this is the normal opening structure of NQ
Most spikes fail and revert 71.3% return to session_open within 60 bars The behavioral insight (trapped participants fueling reversion) is valid
Reversions are fast Median 4 bars to session_open (among reverting sessions) When the reversal happens, it happens quickly — a clean pattern
Confirmation entry is the wrong frame All entry conditions place the trade too close to TP-A Any confirmation that the reversal has started leaves too little room to TP
The SL cannot be moved inside the spike Structural: SL at spike extreme is the definition of "failed spike" A tighter SL would not be testing the spike failure hypothesis — it would be a different strategy

The geometry problem, explained

A failed spike trade requires three prices: the spike extreme (E), the session open (O), and the entry price (P). For the hypothesis to make sense, the SL must be beyond E (if price returns to E, the spike has not failed) and the TP must be at or beyond O (the reversal target).

But any confirmation-based entry — one that waits for evidence the reversal has started — places P between E and O. This makes distance(P→SL) = P−E, and distance(P→TP-A) = O−P. Since P is between E and O, both distances are positive. But their ratio is determined by how close P is to O.

Entries that are close to O (TP nearby, SL far) give poor R/R. Entries that are close to E (SL nearby, TP far) give good R/R but are the earliest possible confirmation — before the reversal is clearly established. This is not a parameter tuning problem. It is the fundamental tradeoff of a confirmation-based entry on a reversal pattern.

What a different approach might look like

These are not planned research phases — they are directions suggested by the Phase 17 findings.

Prediction-based entry

Enter before or at the spike forms — not after. Requires predicting which direction the spike will go. Different hypothesis: "morning conditions predict spike direction," not "spike failure produces reversion."

SL inside the spike range

Place SL at a fraction of spike distance (e.g., 50% retrace) rather than beyond the extreme. Better R/R but the hypothesis changes: you're no longer testing "failure" — price returning to SL level does not disprove the move.

Minimum R/R filter

Only enter when entry price is below some threshold (e.g., within 5 pts of spike extreme). Forces better R/R but the 12.2% of signals with R/R >2.0 (TP-B) would need WR >33% — feasible but requires a separate validation.

Same pattern, different instrument

ES (S&P 500 futures) has lower volatility and narrower spikes. The same methodology might produce entry prices farther from TP-A, improving R/R without changing the hypothesis.

The pre-P&L diagnostic worked exactly as intended. By computing theoretical expectancy before running any backtests, the spec correctly halted 7 unnecessary experiments. This saved compute and — more importantly — prevented a false positive from slippage/cost hiding a structurally negative signal. Negative expectancy before costs means negative expectancy after costs, every time.
Research conclusion

The failed opening spike pattern is behaviorally real and statistically robust in NQ. It cannot be extracted profitably with a confirmation-based entry strategy because the confirmation requirement creates structural negative R/R. This is not a failure of the research methodology — it is the methodology working correctly, stopping before wasted effort and correctly characterizing the nature of the problem.
